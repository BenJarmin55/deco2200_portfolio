This is a process journal page which outlines the design challenges encountered while designing a UI for an autonomous vehicle service.

Week 4

Week 4 became a challenge to integrate all the components which were needed to produce the Task 1 report. The findings from Conextual Observations and an Afinity Diagram along with the other reseach methods were combined.

Figure 4.1 - Contextual Observations of the school pick-up zone
Figure 4.2 - Afinity Diagrams organise observations to bring foundational insights

Early design methods add up to a critical understanding of user context and user needs. With a complex system that is required to impliment AVs in a real world setting, some very critical considerations must be understood and factored into the processes that are needed, and regulatory frameworks will need to oversee.

Afinity diagrams allow experimental groupings which can reveal both potential relations and also real relations between aspects of the complete experience of users and stakeholders in designing interactive systems.My diagram was fairly easy to accomplish, and insights were conveyed to the next stage.

I was tasked to produce the mindmapping of the two user journeys we decided on.

After we evaluated our three ieads we dicarded the shared bus concept, finding it to be impractical as a service and too simple to iterate the required number of screens for the UI. Our mindmapping was applied to two design ideas, Idea 1 which was the journey from the parent's point of view, the requester, and Idea 2 the journey from the child's point of view, the passenger.

Week 8

Week 8 continued testing and annotating the 1st iteration of our low fidelity prototype screens. We then developed medium fidelity SKETCH screens, and retested them after making assessments and adjustments, running them in InVision on mobile phones and desktops, and in a variety of user contexts.

Figure 8.1 - Medium fidelity prototype screens with linked touch areas in blue
Figure 8.2 - Usability testing of the mdium fidelity prototype in a car.

Usability testing of the medium fidelity prototype screens and flow involved a number of locations and contexts.

Some of the development and preparitory testing work was done through online coordination on social media platforms like Google Docs, and Slack, and sometimes a group mobile phone call for up to an hour. Tese methods of group work were supplimentary to our in person meetings. These online methods do not generally work well for me as a designer, but we managed through some very tight scheduling, and sending images, and annotations back and forth. Collabortion is a keystone of large projects, and as with other design projects, developing UI for AVs will need a lot of testing to arrive at safe and accountable protocols and processes for them to be accepted by the wider community.

Charles and Angi concentrated on the images of screens, and the presentation slides, while I reviewed and edited the earlier draft of the report. Editing was proceedural and I introduced phrases in bold text, so they would stand out. During Week 11 the the Amigos began to regroup, and look at acting on our 'next Steps' from the Evaluation Report. This meant locating an expert user to discuss our flow through and general design feedback. Also we set out to create a distinctive visual design, and prearrange some new UI testing for the week 12 Iterative prototype submission.

Figure 11.1 - Compiling the Process Journal

I had done some preliminary work on keeping some notes on the week by week decisions and activities for the journal, but underestimated how much time it would take to find a working computer in the Wilkinson building, which had all the licences for programs like Sublime, Sketch InVision and such. This was a minor factor but frustrating to say the least. I relied on help from tutors when coding problems arose, and all but a few were resolved.

Previous Next

1. Compiling the process journal, though not strictly part of the prototype tasks helped to keep a tab on progrss and timing of our development process.

2. Unifying the visual design was a process of early discussion, settling on colours and font, layout, margins came first. Then each Amigo doing some revision, the passing them on, and a final group discussion. Relying on each others strengths was welcome and helpful to a smooth process.

3. As we progressed through the process, a key UI insight arose. This was that the Trip setup and settings, (not shown), were not something the requester wanted to spend time doing, in comparison with 'facetime, talk or chat' on the app. So deciding what information needed to go on what tab, became a priority, which allowed the elimination of another screen.

I allocated many hours to code this journal. It is not my strong point in contemporary design skills, even though I find it rewarding when I succeed.
